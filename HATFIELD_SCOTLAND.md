# HATFIELD SCOTLAND

---

## THE OPENING PRINCIPLE

**Hatfield Scotland is a new-build distillery in Perthshire producing Scottish gin now and Scotch single malt whisky later. The long play. The American bourbon company putting down roots in Highland whisky country.**

This is not a vanity purchase. Not a flag-planted-for-press-coverage move. It is a genuinely significant investment in a second spirits tradition — one that will take years to pay off in the way it's supposed to. The gin is excellent and commercially useful now. The Scotch will be worth the wait. Still House Scotland sits on the distillery grounds, the second location of the Hatfield fine dining concept.

David Buckner made the case to Eleanor in 2022. The Edinburgh Inn was performing. Scotland liked Hatfield. The whisky industry was consolidating and there was land available with the right water access. Eleanor agreed: if Hatfield is going to be a serious global spirits company, it needs to understand Scotch from the inside.

## THE STORY

**Why Scotland.** The Hatfield brand is built on the idea that where water comes from matters — that the geology beneath a distillery shapes what comes out of it. In Kentucky that's limestone. In Perthshire it's granite and peat-filtered Highland spring water. Different geology, different mineral profile, entirely different whisky character. The argument for Scotland was never "let's make Scotch" — it was "let's understand a second water, a second tradition, and build something serious in it."

**Why Perthshire.** Proximity to Edinburgh (where Hatfield already has an Inn) gives the distillery a practical visitor infrastructure without being in the crowded Speyside tourist circuit. The Highland classification is legitimate: Perthshire sits in the southern Highlands, producing malts with a distinct character — lighter than Speyside, less peated than Islay, rounder than the northern coast. Blair Athol and Edradour are nearby neighbours. Good company. The water source — a granite spring on the purchased land — is exceptional.

**The new build.** Hatfield chose new build over acquisition deliberately. Acquiring an existing distillery means acquiring its character, its reputation, its production constraints, its history. A new build means Fiona MacAllister specifies the stills, the washbacks, the warehouse design, the mash tun. She is making a distillery that produces the whisky she wants to make — not working around someone else's infrastructure. The capital cost was higher. The creative control is complete.

**The timeline reality.** Scotch whisky requires a minimum three years of maturation in oak barrels before it can legally be called Scotch. Hatfield began filling barrels in 2025. The earliest legal release is 2028 — and Fiona has no intention of releasing at three years. She is filling for a 10-year programme. The first serious Hatfield Perthshire Single Malt releases will arrive in the mid-2030s. In the meantime: gin funds the distillery, the tourism experience funds operations, and the barrels do their work.

## THE DISTILLERY

**Architecture:** Stone and slate, integrated into the Perthshire landscape. Not a corporate building dropped into the countryside — a working distillery that looks like it belongs there. The architect worked with traditional Highland distillery forms: pitched roofs, pagoda-style ventilation (non-functional but respectful), stone facing. Inside: modern. The production equipment is new, specified by Fiona, optimised for the malt programme.

**Water source:** On-site granite spring. Tested extensively before purchase — the mineral profile was a deciding factor. Cold, soft, granite-filtered. The opposite of Kentucky limestone water in character. Both are exceptional in their own way.

**Stills:** Two copper pot stills (wash still and spirit still). Tall necks — Fiona is making for elegance and lightness, not heaviness. The still height encourages copper contact and removes heavier compounds. The character she's building for is floral, fruity, accessible but complex.

**Fermentation:** Six Douglas fir washbacks. Longer fermentation times than most Highland distilleries — Fiona allows 90+ hours. The extended ferment develops esters and fruity complexity that carry through distillation.

**Warehousing:** Two traditional dunnage warehouses (low-roofed, earth floor, high humidity). Fiona chose dunnage over racked warehouses: slower maturation, more even temperature, more gentle oak interaction. The spirit will take longer to develop — that's the point.

**Casks:** Primarily ex-bourbon barrels (Hatfield's own spent casks, shipped from Bardstown — the connection to Kentucky made tangible). Some ex-sherry hogsheads and ex-wine casks for complexity. Fiona is experimenting with ex-Hatfield wine casks (Napa and Tuscany) for a small-batch programme — the cross-division conversation made physical.

## HATFIELD HIGHLAND GIN

The immediate commercial programme. Gin doesn't require ageing — Hatfield Scotland could sell gin from day one of production. It funds the Scotch programme and establishes the distillery's presence in market before the malt releases arrive.

**Hatfield Highland Gin** (43.5% ABV)
- London Dry style base, Highland water, botanical programme developed by Rory Irvine.
- **Core botanicals:** Juniper (Bulgarian), coriander seed, angelica root, citrus peel (fresh lemon and orange from the Tuscany estate — a Hatfield cross-division detail), heather flower, thistle, orris root.
- **Still:** Vapour infusion through a Carter-head still (separate from the whisky pot stills — a dedicated gin still). Lighter, more delicate botanical extraction than direct infusion.
- **Profile:** Floral, citrus-forward, heather in the background, clean juniper finish. Distinctly Scottish in character without being heavy. Works beautifully in a highball with Limestone Springs Premium Tonic.
- **Price:** ~£38-42 / $48-55
- **Distribution:** UK national (launch market), US (through Hatfield distribution network), EU growing. Every Hatfield venue serves it.
- **Volume:** ~18,000 cases/year (scaling up)

**Hatfield Highland Gin — Cask Rested** (46% ABV)
- The same gin, 3 months in ex-Hatfield bourbon casks. Adds warmth, vanilla, light amber colour.
- More food-friendly, more complex. Negroni and Old Fashioned adjacent.
- **Price:** ~£48-55 / $60-70
- **Distribution:** More limited — The Stave, specialty retailers, direct.
- **Volume:** ~4,000 cases/year

**Hatfield Perthshire Single Malt** *(anticipated)*
- Production underway. Barrels filling since 2025.
- First legal release possible 2028 (3-year minimum) — Fiona will not release at three years.
- Target release window: 2030 for a 5-year expression; 2035 for the first 10-year.
- Anticipated profile: Floral, green apple, light honey, gentle vanilla from the ex-bourbon casks, heather in the background. Highland in character — not peated, not heavy.
- No price or allocation established. The industry is watching.

## STILL HOUSE SCOTLAND

The second Still House location. Same brand, same philosophy as Bardstown — a serious restaurant on distillery grounds — but a completely different menu, reflecting the Perthshire larder rather than Kentucky's.

**Location:** Within the distillery complex. Purpose-built alongside the main distillery building. Stone and timber, the same architectural language as the distillery. Views across the Perthshire hills.

**Chef:** Catriona Reid. Scottish-born, trained in Edinburgh (Martin Wishart's kitchen), staged in Copenhagen and Paris. She was chosen by Marcus Boyle in Bardstown after a dinner at which Buckner was present — Boyle's recommendation carries weight. Catriona wanted to run something ambitious in Scotland without going to London. This was the offer.

**Menu philosophy:** The Scottish larder is exceptional and underused at this level. Catriona cooks what Perthshire produces — Highland beef, venison, game, river salmon, seasonal vegetables from kitchen garden on site, foraged herbs and mushrooms — with French technique and Nordic restraint. No Burns Night clichés. No haggis on the menu unless the season and the supply justify it. A serious restaurant that happens to be in Scotland, rather than a Scottish restaurant doing the expected things.

**Spirits integration:** Hatfield Highland Gin features prominently — gin-cured salmon, gin and tonic sorbet, botanical oil in savoury dishes where it earns it. The Scotch barrels, when they're ready, will become a Still House Scotland menu event. Hatfield bourbon and the full whisky range available at the bar.

**Scale:** Seats 45 inside, 20 on a covered terrace overlooking the distillery and hills. Open Tuesday–Sunday, dinner only in winter; lunch and dinner spring–autumn. Reservations essential — advance booking culture is the same as Bardstown. The location is more remote than Still House Bardstown, which filters the clientele naturally: the people who come to Still House Scotland made a decision to come.

**Target recognition:** Marcus Boyle has talked to Catriona about a Michelin campaign. The food merits it. The remoteness is a variable — Michelin inspectors travel, but remoteness can delay recognition. Buckner considers it a medium-term ambition.

## VISITOR EXPERIENCE

The distillery is open for tours and tastings. More visitor-infrastructure than Bardstown (which is a working facility that tolerates tours) — Scotland's whisky tourism is an industry in itself, and a new distillery in Perthshire can reasonably expect significant visitor traffic.

**Distillery Tour:** 90 minutes. Takes visitors through the full production process — mash tun, washbacks, stills, warehouses. Includes a three-dram tasting: Hatfield Highland Gin, Hatfield Highland Gin Cask Rested, and a new-make spirit (unaged, straight from the still — gives visitors a sense of what the Scotch will eventually become). £25 per person. Advance booking recommended.

**Premium Tasting:** 2.5 hours. Small group (max 8). More depth on process, more drams, barrel tasting from the warehouse (filling direct from cask). Includes lunch at Still House Scotland. £120 per person.

**Private Hire:** The distillery event space (capacity 60) available for corporate and private events. Full Hatfield hospitality programme.

## NARRATOR RULES

**Do not explain the distillery in scene.** If Alex visits, or if Hatfield Scotland comes up, treat it like any other Hatfield property — a place, not a concept. The context is in this file.

**The gin is the current product.** The Scotch is not yet available. Do not write scenes where Alex or anyone drinks Hatfield Scotch whisky before 2030 at the earliest.

**Still House Scotland is not Still House Bardstown.** Different menu, different chef, different landscape, same brand philosophy. Don't conflate them.
