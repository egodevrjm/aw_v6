# THE STAVE

---

## THE OPENING PRINCIPLE

**The Stave is a private members' club where whiskey and music culture intersect — built around exceptional spirits, curated programming, and listening rooms that let recorded music sound the way it was meant to.**

This is the jewel in the Hatfield Group crown. Not the biggest revenue generator (that's the Bar & Grill chain), not the oldest operation (that's the distillery), but the one with the most cachet, the most distinctive concept, and the deepest cultural positioning. The Stave exists because Hatfield believed the right room for drinking serious bourbon was also the right room for listening to serious music — and built an institution around that premise.

Two locations open: Soho, London (the original, est. 2015) and Tribeca, NYC (est. 2022). Tokyo planned for 2027, Paris for 2028. Six to eight total at full buildout — deliberately limited. Membership is selective, expensive, and slow. This is not Soho House. This is not a celebrity lounge. It is a serious private club for people with genuine connections to music, spirits, and culture.

Alex has family access. He uses The Stave London when he's in Soho and wants a quiet evening with world-class bourbon and a listening room that makes music feel alive. He doesn't manage it, doesn't get involved, doesn't trade on it. It's a room he can walk into because of his last name — and a room he appreciates because of his ear.

## THE STORY

**2012-2014, the idea:** Eleanor and David Buckner were looking for a way to extend the Hatfield brand into a premium, experience-driven format that went beyond restaurants and hotels. The bourbon market was growing globally. London was drinking more American whiskey than ever before. Members' clubs were booming in Soho — but none had bourbon at their centre, and none had a music proposition that went beyond background playlists.

The concept that emerged was specific: a club where the connection between whiskey and music wasn't decorative but structural. Bourbon and music share something — craft, time, the way both reward attention. The Stave would be built around that. A serious whiskey programme, a serious music programme, and rooms that took both seriously. Buckner commissioned acoustic engineers to design a listening room to the highest specifications — a room where recorded music would sound the way it did in the studio. Not background music. Not a stage. A room built to listen.

Everything else was built around that room.

**2015:** The Stave London opened in Soho. A converted Georgian townhouse on a quiet street off Dean Street. Four floors. Bourbon bar, dining room, event space, and — on the second floor, behind heavy doors — the listening room.

**2022:** The Stave NYC opened in Tribeca. A converted warehouse. Different architecture, different energy, same proposition: bourbon, food, music, and a listening room built to the same acoustic standard.

## THE STAVE — LONDON (SOHO)

### Location & Building

**Address area:** Quiet street off Dean Street, Soho. Not on the main drag — you have to know it's there. Unmarked entrance with a small brass plaque. Georgian townhouse, four storeys, narrow frontage, deep interior.

### Layout

**Ground floor — The Bar:**
- The heart of the club. Long mahogany bar, 14 seats. Low lighting, leather seating, exposed brick.
- Bourbon programme: 200+ expressions. Full Hatfield range including Cave Select (members-only allocation). American whiskey, Scotch, Irish, Japanese — curated, not encyclopaedic. Every bottle on the back bar has been tasted and selected by the bar team.
- Cocktail programme: Classic and original. The Old Fashioned at The Stave is a reference standard — Hatfield Single Barrel, Demerara, Angostura, expressed orange peel, hand-cut ice. Nothing fussy.
- Seating: Bar stools, leather armchairs, two booth alcoves. Capacity ~45.
- Atmosphere: Warm, low, conversational. Music playing but not dominating. The bar is for talking and drinking.

**Ground floor — The Dining Room (rear):**
- 40 seats. Seasonal menu, modern British with bourbon influence where it makes sense (bourbon-glazed pork belly, whiskey barrel-aged steak, smoked dishes). Head Chef runs a serious kitchen — this is not bar food.
- Average dinner: £65-85 per head before drinks.
- Reservations required for dinner. Members only (no guest-of-a-guest for dining).
- Private dining alcove (8 seats) available for booking.

**First floor — The Drawing Room:**
- Lounge space. Sofas, armchairs, low tables, bookshelves. The room for afternoon meetings, quiet work, reading.
- Coffee service from 10am. Afternoon tea available. Bar service from 5pm.
- Capacity ~35.
- The quietest room in the club. No music. The silence is the point.

**Second floor — The Listening Room:**
- See dedicated section below. This is the room Homer specified.
- 32 seats. Scheduled listening sessions + private bookings.

**Third floor — Events & Private Hire:**
- Flexible event space. Capacity ~60 standing, ~35 seated.
- Bourbon tasting events, album launch evenings, acoustic performances, industry gatherings.
- Available for member private hire.

### The Listening Room — London

**The defining feature of every Stave location.**

A purpose-built room for listening to recorded music properly. Not a concert venue. Not a DJ booth. Not a hi-fi showroom. A room where the acoustics, the speakers, the seating geometry, and the sound treatment have been specified to the highest standard — built so that music sounds the way it was intended to in the studio.

**Specifications:**
- **Dimensions:** Proportioned to avoid standing waves and flutter echo. Ratios specified by the acoustic engineering team — derived from the mathematics of studio design rather than standard architectural convention.
- **Walls:** Multi-layer construction. Dense inner wall, air gap, absorptive treatment, diffusion panels at calculated intervals. The room doesn't echo. It doesn't deaden. It breathes.
- **Ceiling:** Variable height with integrated diffusion. Prevents ceiling reflections from smearing the stereo image.
- **Floor:** Hardwood over isolated subfloor. Vibration decoupled from the building structure.
- **Speakers:** Custom installation. Concealed behind acoustically transparent fabric panels. The specific make is not public — drivers, crossover points, and placement specified by the acoustic team. Installed by specialist engineers, not a commercial AV company.
- **Amplification:** Separate from the club's general audio. Dedicated signal chain from source to speaker. No Bluetooth. No streaming compression. Physical media (vinyl, CD, high-resolution digital files from a dedicated server) only.
- **Seating:** 32 seats arranged in a calculated geometry. Every seat is a good seat — no "sweet spot" that makes the rest of the room second-class. The room is designed so that a listener at seat 1 and seat 32 both hear the music correctly.
- **Lighting:** Dimmable to near-darkness during sessions. The listening room is an eyes-closed experience.

**How it operates:**
- **Curated sessions** (3-4 per week, evenings): A Listening Director programmes themed evenings — full album plays, genre deep-dives, era retrospectives, new release premieres. No talking during playback. Bourbon served before and after. Capacity 32. Booking required.
- **Open listening** (select afternoons): The room is open for members to sit and listen. The Listening Director or staff select the music. Still no talking — the room has rules.
- **Private bookings:** Members can book the listening room for private sessions. Bring your own vinyl. Request from the archive. The room is available with or without a guided experience.
- **Artist sessions** (by invitation): Occasionally, artists or producers premiere work in The Stave listening room. These are private, word-of-mouth events. Not marketed. Not photographed. A producer who has new work plays it in the room Homer designed, for an audience of 32 people who understand what they're hearing.

**What it sounds like:**
- If you've never heard music in a properly treated room, The Stave listening room is a revelation. Instruments separate. Bass is felt, not just heard. Vocals exist in physical space. The experience is closer to being in the studio during playback than anything else outside a professional control room.
- If you have heard music in a properly treated room — if you're Homer, or someone like him — The Stave listening room is one of the best rooms in the world for its purpose.

**Listening Director (London):** Full-time position. Someone with deep music knowledge, genuine audiophile sensibility, and the ability to programme compelling listening sessions. Not a DJ. Not a performer. A curator.

## THE STAVE — NYC (TRIBECA)

### Location & Building

**Address area:** Tribeca. Converted warehouse on a quiet block. Larger footprint than London — wider floors, higher ceilings, different energy. Industrial-warm aesthetic (exposed brick, steel beams, timber floors) compared to London's Georgian intimacy.

### Layout

**Ground floor — The Bar & Lounge:**
- Larger bar than London (22 seats). Same bourbon programme depth — 200+ expressions, full Hatfield range, Cave Select allocation.
- Lounge seating area with cocktail service. Capacity ~60.
- Open-plan feel. Higher ceilings give the room air that London's Georgian proportions don't allow.
- Atmosphere: Tribeca energy — slightly more kinetic than London, still sophisticated. Tuesday night feels like an industry living room.

**Ground floor — The Restaurant:**
- 55 seats. American-forward menu (versus London's modern British). Serious kitchen — steak programme, seasonal fish, bourbon-forward preparations where they earn it.
- Average dinner: $80-110 per head before drinks.
- Open for lunch (rare for a private club — NYC members wanted it). Power lunch crowd on weekdays.

**Second floor — The Listening Room:**
- Built to the same Hatfield acoustic specification as London, adapted for different architectural constraints (higher ceiling, wider space, different wall construction).
- 36 seats (slightly larger than London — the room dimensions allowed it without compromising acoustics).
- Same operational model: curated sessions, open listening, private bookings, artist sessions.
- Different Listening Director than London (each location has its own).

**Second floor — The Library:**
- Quiet space with music reference library. Books, magazines, vinyl collection.
- NYC's equivalent of London's Drawing Room but with a vinyl-browsing element.

**Third floor — Events & Studio:**
- Flexible event space (~80 standing, ~45 seated).
- Small recording capability — not a professional studio, but a room with decent acoustics and equipment for intimate acoustic sessions, podcast recording, interviews. Functional, not world-class. A member amenity.

### NYC Membership Profile

Different from London. NYC skews:
- Music industry (labels, management, producers, publishers). Tribeca is close to enough of them.
- Finance with serious music taste (the Goldman partner who builds speaker systems in his apartment).
- Media and culture (writers, editors, film/TV people who care about sound).
- Tech (audio tech, streaming platform people, music tech founders).
- Less old-money establishment than London. More active industry.

## MEMBERSHIP

### Who Gets In

The Stave is selective. Not cruel, not snobby, but selective. The membership committee (3 people per location, appointed by the General Manager) evaluates applications on:

1. **Connection to music, spirits, or culture.** Not "do you like music" but "do you have a genuine relationship to it?" A record producer, a bourbon educator, a music journalist, a serious collector, a working musician — these are natural members. A hedge fund manager who likes to party is not.
2. **Character.** Will this person respect the listening room rules? Will they treat staff well? Will they contribute to the atmosphere?
3. **Sponsorship.** Two existing members must sponsor each application. This keeps the membership self-selecting.

**Who you find inside:**
- Music industry professionals (producers, A&R, engineers, managers, publishers)
- Spirits industry professionals (distillers, brand directors, bar owners, spirits writers)
- Cultural figures (journalists, authors, filmmakers, photographers) with genuine music or spirits connections
- Business people with genuine taste (the founder who built a speaker company, the investor who runs a jazz label, the architect who designs listening rooms)
- A few genuinely wealthy people who are there because they care about the programme, not the status

**Who you don't find inside:**
- Celebrities using it as a scene
- Influencers looking for content
- People who joined for the business card and never use the listening room
- Anyone who talks during listening sessions (they get spoken to, and then they stop, or they leave)

## EXPANSION ROADMAP

The Stave is the Hatfield division with the most international expansion potential. Private members' clubs are a proven global format. Bourbon is globally ascendant. Homer's listening room concept is unique and replicable (the acoustic specifications can be adapted to different architectural contexts).

### Planned Locations

**Tokyo** *(target: 2027)*
- Japanese whisky culture + audiophile culture = natural Stave market
- Likely location: Roppongi or Azabu-Juban. Intimate, exclusive.
- The listening room concept will resonate deeply in Japan — the country already has a "jazz kissa" tradition of dedicated listening bars. The Stave elevates this to private club format with world-class acoustics.
- Bourbon programme + Japanese whisky selection as a bridge.

**Paris** *(target: 2028)*
- Le Marais or Saint-Germain. French cocktail culture and American spirits appreciation are both strong.
- Listening room concept adapts well — Paris has serious audiophile and music collector communities.
- Eleanor's French connections through Rosie's European network may facilitate this.

**Los Angeles** *(under evaluation)*
- West Hollywood or Silver Lake. Music industry density makes LA an obvious market.
- Question: does LA need a Stave when the music industry is already saturated with private spaces? Answer may be yes — nothing in LA combines bourbon, Homer-grade acoustics, and genuine selectivity.

**Dubai** *(longer-term evaluation)*
- Luxury private club market. Bourbon programme would adapt. Listening room concept is unique in the market.
- Alcohol licensing and cultural considerations require careful navigation.

### The Listening Room Specification

Each new Stave location requires a Homer-specified listening room. Homer's original specifications are documented and held by Hatfield Group. The acoustic principles are adaptable to different room shapes and building structures — Homer designed for the physics, not for one specific room.

For new locations, the process:
1. Architects design the space
2. Homer's listening room specifications are applied to the new room dimensions
3. Acoustic engineers (from Homer's trusted network) adapt the treatment, speaker placement, and seating geometry
4. Installation and calibration

Homer is not personally involved in new installations (he's 83 and at the compound). The specifications exist as a detailed technical document. The trusted engineers execute them. Homer may review plans if asked, but the system is designed to work without his ongoing involvement.

## NARRATOR RULE — DO NOT EXPLAIN THE STAVE IN SCENE

**The Stave is a private members club. When it appears in scene, treat it like Annabel's or Zero Bond — a place, not a concept.**

Don't explain the listening room, the bourbon focus, the Homer connection, or the membership model. Characters who are there already know what it is. Characters who aren't there don't need the brochure.

**Wrong:**
> They went to The Stave — his family's private members club in Soho, built around a Homer-specified listening room and a serious bourbon programme.

**Right:**
> They went to The Stave. Alex had the booth by the listening room.

If the listening room matters to the scene, show it. Don't introduce it.
