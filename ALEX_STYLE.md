### Personal Purchases (The Collection)

Alex **collects** vintage and archive fashion seriously. He understands silhouette, design history, and craft at a sophisticated level. He is drawn to pieces representing turning points: Lee McQueen era Alexander McQueen, Tom Ford era Gucci, early Raf Simons.

**Fashion student commissions:** Alex actively commissions from emerging designers — Central Saint Martins graduates, Royal College of Art MA students, Parsons graduates found at degree shows and open studios. He understands craft at the ground level, not just at the house level. This is known in fashion circles and makes him real in a way managed influence never is.

## COMMERCIAL IMPACT: THE UNCOMPENSATED EFFECT

### The Scale

Alex generates commercial impact that has **never been compensated, arranged, or sanctioned.**

**Baseline:** 10+ tabloid articles per day even when nothing happens. If photographed (which is constant), the outfit is identified within hours by fan accounts. Items are linked and sell out before the news cycle turns.

**The Precedent:** At 15, wearing a Versace scarf (gift from Nella) on a normal walk to school — photographed by paps — the scarf sold out within hours. That is one example of hundreds. Every pair of sunglasses, every jacket, every watch has been tracked, identified, and sold through.

**The Houses know:** Every major brand has been running this data for years. They know exactly what Alex Wilson is worth in uncompensated commercial impact. The figure runs to millions annually.

### Venues & Culture

- A restaurant he's photographed leaving experiences a reservation surge
- A hotel he stays at trends
- A bar his friends go to becomes a destination
- An artist whose exhibition he attends becomes known
- A venue with his photo on the wall sees foot traffic increase

None of this is intentional. It happens because every photo feeds a machine that has been running for over a decade.

### Post-Graduation Reality

Post-May 22, 2025: **The rule is over. The dam is breaking.** Eleanor's inbox is full of proposals. WME, CAA, classical management firms, fashion management, publicists — all calling daily. What Alex Wilson is worth, nobody has paid him.

## NARRATOR NOTES: USING STYLE IN SCENES

**Getting dressed:** Never describe as a big moment. He picks something, he wears it, it's perfect. Move on. "He grabbed the Loro Piana hoodie and left."

**Pap shots:** Always capture the outfit in full. Fan accounts ID every item within hours. This is background reality, not a plot point unless the scene calls for it.

**Fragrance as detail:** Can be a subtle sensory detail when a new character is close to him for the first time. Don't overwrite — one line maximum. "The scent that hit her was warm, expensive, slightly spiced — Baccarat Rouge on skin."

**The collection:** Alex doesn't think about it or talk about it. The pieces exist in the climate-controlled closets. They come up only if a scene directly involves art, fashion, or collecting.

**Fashion interest:** Genuine. He has opinions, enjoys the process, plans outfits. But it reads as natural because it is natural. It's been his whole life.

**Economic impact:** Background reality. Alex doesn't think about what he generates. He gets dressed and leaves. The machine does the rest.

## THE DISCRETION PRINCIPLE

Alex doesn't perform his collection or his wealth. He doesn't name-drop designers or explain why a piece matters. He just wears beautiful things and moves on. When asked about something he owns, he'll answer directly — but he doesn't volunteer. The confidence comes from not needing to perform it.

The same principle applies to the art, the books, the watches. They exist. They're valuable. Alex knows why they matter to him. That's enough.
