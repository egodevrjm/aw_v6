# CUT SCENE GUIDE

**The CUT SCENE command shifts the narrator out of Alex's POV entirely. It shows the world reacting, operating, and continuing without him present. This document covers: what CUT SCENES are for, how they work, and the full roster of groups and spaces they can access.**

---

## WHAT A CUT SCENE IS

A CUT SCENE is always non-Alex POV. It shows:

- A conversation continuing after Alex leaves the room
- A group chat, Slack channel, or professional thread reacting to something Alex did
- A Zoom call or meeting Alex isn't in
- Social media activity Alex isn't watching
- The world processing an event through a lens Alex doesn't have access to

**The information ceiling applies absolutely.** A CUT SCENE can only show characters reacting to what they actually know. A private studio session doesn't appear in Legacy until Alex posts about it or gets papped leaving. A meeting with WME doesn't appear in the Sayre Boys chat until Alex tells Tommy. The CUT SCENE shows the world as it actually is — not omniscient, bounded by what reached each group.

---

## USE CASES

### 1. THE ROOM AFTER HE LEAVES
Alex finishes a meeting, a session, a conversation — and walks out. The CUT SCENE shows what happens next. Eleanor calls someone. The WME team debrief. Homer plays the recording back alone and says one thing to Billy in the studio. The room has a reaction it didn't have while Alex was in it.

### 2. PROFESSIONAL/INDUSTRY CHANNELS
Slack channels, internal threads, agency group chats. A signing. A video going viral. Eleanor's office reacting before the public cascade hits. Deutsche Grammophon's A&R team. WME's internal channel after a meeting. These are corporate/professional spaces with their own register — businesslike, fast, occasionally unguarded.

### 3. ZOOM CALLS AND CONFERENCE CALLS
Eleanor briefing the Walker Holdings team. A label call. The Greystone security team running a threat assessment. A PR crisis call. Characters who would never meet in person in the same room, reacting to the same event from different positions.

### 4. SOCIAL MEDIA WITHOUT ALEX
Fan accounts posting. Reddit threads. TikTok comments. Twitter/X reactions. Classical music Twitter losing its mind. The tabloid comment sections. These are public spaces — the narrator renders them through the media-renderer skill, but the CUT SCENE command triggers the shift to that POV.

### 5. GROUP CHATS ALEX ISN'T IN
The most useful CUT SCENE territory. See full roster below.

### 🎵 THE KNIGHTS OF THE MIXING DESK
**Platform:** WhatsApp | **Size:** 5-6 | **Named by:** Rosie (they didn't name it themselves)

**Members:** Sir Homer Wilson · Sir Paul McCartney · Sir Elton John · Sir Mick Jagger · Sir Brian May · Sir Barry Gibb

**What it is:** Six men who have been friends since the 1960s and 70s, all now in their late 70s and 80s, all knighted, all still working. Arguments about technical things that have been running since 1968 — the bass EQ disagreement between Homer and Paul is ongoing. When something happens to one of them, the others show up immediately and without fanfare.

**CUT SCENE use:** Homer shares the studio recording of Alex playing something. The reaction from five men who collectively represent the most significant body of recorded music in history — hearing something that might be the next generation of what they built — is not a small thing. Paul asks one question. Elton cries (doesn't say so, but it's obvious). Mick says something dry and correct. Homer says nothing and that says everything.

## CUT SCENE RENDERING: TYPES AND VOICE

---

### TYPE 1: MEDIA REACTION CUT SCENE

**What happens:** A news cycle or media event reacts to something Alex did. The pap photos land. The video goes viral. An article publishes.

**Voice:** Media-renderer skill. Tabloid articles, entertainment journalism, social media posts, fan account threads, comment sections. The register is journalistic (tabloids), parasocial (fans), and institutional (press).

**Time frame:** Usually compressed. Multiple outlets reacting in real time. The spread is rapid.

**Information constraint:** Only what's visible in the event itself. A pap photo shows what's in the frame — nothing more. A viral video shows the clip — context is fans' interpretation.

---

### TYPE 2: INDUSTRY REACTION CUT SCENE

**What happens:** The business world reacts. Eleanor's Slack fires up. Labels call. Agents strategize. The machine moves.

**Voice:** Business-renderer skill. Professional register. Fast. Occasional technical jargon. The tone is urgent without panic.

**Time frame:** Same-day or next-morning response. Industry moves faster than media.

**Information constraint:** Industry people know more than the public, but they only know what they're told or what they've pieced together from calls and leaks.

---

### TYPE 3: INNER CIRCLE REACTION CUT SCENE

**What happens:** The people closest to Alex react. Rosie and Homer. The Sayre Boys. The London Lot. The people who know him.

**Voice:** Intimate. Warm or concerned. The register of people who know each other deeply. Occasionally irreverent — these are his friends, not his publicists.

**Time frame:** Whenever they find out. Usually hours to a day after an event, depending on how the information reaches them.

**Information constraint:** These people know Alex himself, but they don't always know what he knows about the situation. Misunderstandings are real.

---

### TYPE 4: PROFESSIONAL/ORGANIZATIONAL CUT SCENE

**What happens:** A meeting happens. A discussion occurs. The room after Alex leaves.

**Voice:** Depends on the group. A WME conference call is different from a studio session debrief is different from a security team threat assessment. The register shifts with the context.

**Time frame:** Real time or immediately after.

**Information constraint:** What people in the room know, discussed face-to-face or on Zoom.

## NARRATOR NOTES FOR CUT SCENES

- **Always establish what the group knows** before writing their reaction. Run the information state audit from the scene-compositor. A group reacting to something they couldn't know yet breaks the world.
- **Voice register changes completely** outside Alex's POV. Eleanor in the Walker Holdings Slack is not Eleanor in the FAM chat. Rosie in The Session is not Rosie at the kitchen table. Write from where they actually are.
- **The CUT SCENE ends cleanly.** Return to Alex's POV with a new scene header. Do not bleed information from the CUT SCENE into Alex's awareness unless there's a mechanism (he gets a call, someone tells him).
- **The most powerful CUT SCENES are the ones Alex never sees.** Homer playing the recording back alone. The Knights of the Mixing Desk going quiet. The Earl's one-line response. What the world does with Alex when he's not in the room is often more revealing than what it does to his face.
- **Length varies.** A CUT SCENE can be 200 words (a brief Slack thread) or 2,000 words (a full meeting). The length serves the content, not the reverse.
- **Multiple perspectives in one CUT SCENE are OK.** A media CUT SCENE can show tabloid coverage AND fan reactions AND industry Slack in sequence. The key is that none of it is Alex's POV.
