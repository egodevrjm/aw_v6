# THE PHOENIX HOLLOW CIRCLE

**The eight to ten legendary music figures who are Alex's de facto family. Rosie's inner circle. The people who shaped his life from birth.**

---

## WHAT THE CIRCLE IS

The Phoenix Hollow Circle is not a professional arrangement. It is not a networking opportunity or a passing collaboration. These are the people who were in the room when Alex was born. Who watched him learn to play. Who are at the Solstice every summer. Whose opinions on music matter to Alex in a way that no external critic's ever will. Whose lives are woven into the fabric of his life so completely that he has grown up inside their world, not observing it from the outside.

Rosie formed her closest creative relationships in the 1970s and 1980s — with people she made music alongside, who understood what she was trying to do, who never asked her to be anything other than who she was. Those friendships deepened over decades. By the 1990s, they had become an infrastructure of their own: a group of people who gathered at Phoenix Hollow regularly, who knew each other's children, who had shaped the sound of popular music across four decades.

Alex's childhood was shaped by proximity to these people. Not in a performed way — in the truest sense. Stevie Nicks was in the kitchen. Paul McCartney was in the studio. Elton taught him piano tricks. Dolly understood something about performance that mattered. These were not celebrities he occasionally encountered. They were the architecture of his daily life.

## THE INNER CIRCLE

### STEVIE NICKS

**b. 1948 | Age 77 in 2025 | The godmother of Phoenix Hollow.**

Stevie has been a constant in the compound since before Alex was born. She has a preferred cottage on the property (Stone House — east-facing, views of the meadow). She stays for weeks at a time, sometimes longer. The relationship with Rosie dates to the late 1970s — shared Nashville connections, a mutual respect that turned into deep friendship. When Dolly and Stevie arrived in Rosie's orbit, something shifted in the music. They made each other better.

**Her relationship to Rosie:** Equal. Not mentor and student, not elder and younger. Two women who understood each other's musical instincts absolutely and didn't need to explain anything. They harmonise the way people who know each other's breath patterns can harmonise. They talk constantly. The conversations are not always about music — sometimes it's about aging, about how to hold boundaries, about what Stevie calls "the long haul of being visible." Rosie listens more than she talks, which is itself a form of attention.

**Her relationship to Alex:** A kind of musical godmother. She watched him grow up playing, and when he was old enough to play seriously, she sat beside him and listened without judgment. She has played with him (rare, spontaneous moments at Solstice parties when they both ended up at the piano or with guitars). She has opinions about his playing that he genuinely respects. She does not praise him because he is Rosie's grandson. She praised him when he was eight and genuinely good for eight. She praises him now because he is genuinely extraordinary. The distinction matters to him.

**Her presence at the compound:** Stevie moves through the house like she lives here. She has preferred spots (the sunroom in the afternoon, the studio kitchen early morning with coffee). She knows the staff. She knows the rhythms. When Alex was a child and Stevie was in residence, he could walk into the sunroom and she would be there, and she would turn whatever she was doing — reading, writing, thinking — into attention for him. Not performed. Just there.

**What Alex knows about her:** The voice. The music. The way she moves through the world with absolute conviction. The quiet authority she carries that has nothing to do with fame and everything to do with knowing exactly who she is. She has never softened herself for anyone. She has also never been unkind. Alex understands these things because he watched her live them.

**Summers at the Solstice:** Stevie performs almost every year — usually late, when the evening has settled into intimacy. Sometimes it's a song she and Rosie wrote together. Sometimes it's something from deep catalogue that nobody's heard her sing in decades. The quality of silence in the amphitheatre when Stevie plays is a thing unto itself. The people around her understand they are witnessing something that doesn't happen anywhere else.

### DOLLY PARTON

**b. 1946 | Age 79 in 2025 | The voice that taught Alex about performance.**

Dolly is the most frequently at the compound besides Stevie. She and Rosie have a relationship that has weathered everything — different musical worlds, different approaches, different public postures. They love each other simply and without complication. Dolly comes to Kentucky the way other people come home.

**Her musical legacy:** Dolly's impact on music cannot be overstated — the songwriting, the vocal performances, the willingness to evolve without losing her identity. She is also, in a way that matters for Alex, a person who understood performance as a complete expression. Not just the music. The presence, the connection with the audience, the way a room changes when she's in it. These things interested her in a technical way that Rosie, more naturally private, found endlessly fascinating.

**Her relationship to Alex:** Dolly watches performances. She listens the way a craftsperson listens — identifying technique, emotional intention, the thousands of small choices that make a performance land or not. When Alex was young, she would comment on his playing in a way that was both specific and kind. As he got older, the specificity increased. She does not need to tell him he is good. She tells him what he did well and what he might reconsider, which is a form of respect that Alex understands as genuine investment in his development.

**Her presence at the compound:** Less settled than Stevie — Dolly has her own life in Nashville that doesn't leave extended room for Kentucky. But when she comes, she comes fully. She sits at the table. She talks with Homer about production. She plays. She has a way of making everyone around her more themselves — not through charm, but through sheer authenticity. You cannot be false around Dolly.

**What she represents for Alex:** The integration of technical mastery and emotional generosity. The idea that a performer can have complete command of their instrument AND be vulnerable with an audience. That these things are not in conflict. That reaching people and having technical rigor both matter, and you need both.

**At the Solstice:** Dolly almost always performs. The year is incomplete without her voice in the amphitheatre. When she sings with Rosie, it is a kind of conversation between two people who have been talking through music for fifty years.

### ELTON JOHN

**b. 1947 | Age 78 in 2025 | The godfather. The person Alex was named partly for.**

Elton is one of Alex's four official godparents (the others: Donatella Versace, Stella McCartney, Chris Martin). But his role in the Circle is distinct from that formal arrangement. Elton has been at the compound since the early 1980s. He has a cottage (Creek Cottage — he likes the sound of water). He comes in summer for the Solstice, at Christmas, sometimes for extended stretches in spring.

**His musical relationship to Rosie:** Mutual respect bordering on brotherly. They made music together in the 1970s, watched each other build careers that nobody else quite understood, supported each other through industry shifts and personal upheavals. Elton's devotion to Rosie is documented and absolute — he has said, in interviews, that she changed how he understood what a woman in music could be. She has said equally kind things about him.

**His relationship to Homer:** Elton and Homer have a technical conversation that runs alongside their friendship. Elton respects Homer's engineering as much as any producer living. They talk about sound in a language that is native to both of them.

**His relationship to Alex:** The godfather relationship is real and specific. Elton teaches Alex piano tricks — not in a formal lesson context, but in organic moments where they're both at a keyboard and Elton shows him a voicing, a fingering, an approach to a passage that unlocks something. He is genuinely devoted to Alex's musical development. This is not because Alex is Rosie's grandson. This is because Elton sees something in Alex's musicianship that he recognizes and wants to nurture.

**His presence at the compound:** Elton is one of the Circle members most likely to simply be living there for weeks. He has his cottage. He has his routines. He plays the Steinway in the music room. He has breakfast with Rosie. He is family in the most literal sense.

**What he represents:** The integration of greatness and generosity. The idea that you can be one of the greatest musicians who ever lived and still show up to teach a young person, still have genuine curiosity about what they're becoming. That technical excellence and human warmth are not in conflict.

**His god-siblings for Alex:** Zachary (born 2010) and Elijah (born 2013) Furnish-John. Alex is seven and ten years older, respectively. He has known them since they were born. The role is protective and warm — an older brother dynamic more than a godfather dynamic. He has watched them grow up at the compound.

**At the Solstice:** Elton almost always performs. The sound of him at the piano in the amphitheatre is a staple of the event. He plays. He watches. He is present as a participant in the community, not as a guest star.

### PAUL McCARTNEY

**b. 1942 | Age 83 in 2025 | The anchor of the music world.**

Paul's relationship to Phoenix Hollow is particular: he comes less frequently than Stevie or Elton — he has his own life, his own orbit, his own work that doesn't leave endless room. But when he comes, something shifts in the compound. The presence of Paul McCartney carries a weight that even the rest of the Circle acknowledges. Not in a hierarchical way. But in the way that some people's absence makes the room feel different when they're not there, and their presence makes it feel complete when they are.

**His musical relationship to Rosie:** They made music together only once or twice formally, but their respect for each other's work is fundamental. Rosie has covered Paul's songs. Paul has attended Solstice parties where Rosie performed. There is an old-friendship ease between them that suggests they understand each other's approach to music and to the industry in a way that requires no explanation.

**His relationship to Homer:** Paul respects Homer's legacy as an engineer. They have had conversations about the sound of records from different eras, about the evolution of recording technology, about what made certain albums sound the way they did. Homer listens more than he talks, which is his way, but Paul's presence brings something out in him.

**His relationship to Alex:** Paul listens when Alex plays. That is the extent of the formal relationship. But his attention — genuine, knowledgeable, absolutely present — carries weight. When Paul nods after hearing Alex play, it means something. Not because Paul is famous. Because Paul understands music at a depth that Alex respects absolutely.

**His presence at the compound:** Paul doesn't stay as long as Stevie or Elton. He comes for a few days, sometimes a week. He is not as embedded in the daily rhythm. But the days he is there are different. The energy shifts. People who might be semi-engaged become fully attentive. The level of the conversation rises.

**What he represents:** The long view. The idea that you can have a career spanning six decades and still be genuinely curious about music, still listen with full attention, still be interested in what's happening next. That longevity doesn't mean ossification.

**At the Solstice:** Paul doesn't always perform — sometimes he just listens. When he does perform, it is usually with a borrowed instrument, late in the evening, with an ease that suggests he is doing this for the music's sake, not for any other reason. The moment he picks up a guitar is a story that spreads through the circle within minutes.

### DAVID GILMOUR

**b. 1946 | Age 79 in 2025 | The architect of sound.**

David's relationship to Phoenix Hollow is newer than Stevie's or Elton's, but his presence has quickly become essential. He comes less frequently than the inner core, but when he does, Homer and he disappear into the studio for hours at a time. They speak the same language about guitar and about production — a language that is partly about technique and partly about the almost mystical quality of how certain combinations of instruments and recording techniques create particular emotional responses in listeners.

**His musical relationship to Rosie:** Rosie has always respected David's approach to the guitar — the way he prioritizes space and emotion over technical display. They have different sensibilities in some ways, but they share a core conviction that the song matters more than the performance.

**His relationship to Homer:** This is the strongest relationship in the pairing. Homer and David have technical conversations that run deep. They think about sound in compatible ways. When Homer was younger and more active in production, conversations with David would shape how he approached certain albums. Now that Homer is older, David's presence still brings that level of engagement back.

**His relationship to Alex:** David listens to Alex play with the attention of someone who understands what it takes to develop a voice on an instrument. He does not offer much direct feedback. His presence IS the feedback. When David is in the house and Alex is playing, Alex is playing at a different level of intention.

**His presence at the compound:** David is quieter than some of the Circle. He observes. He listens. He talks when he has something specific to say. He and Homer will spend entire days in the studio working through some particular sound problem that they find endlessly interesting.

**At the Solstice:** David sometimes performs, sometimes sits in the amphitheatre with a guitar in hand, sometimes simply listens. His choices seem guided by what feels right in that moment, not by any obligation.

### JONI MITCHELL

**b. 1943 | Age 82 in 2025 | The musical philosopher.**

Joni's presence at Phoenix Hollow carries a particular quality of reverence — not because she demands it, but because everyone in the room understands they are in the presence of one of the greatest songwriters who has ever lived. Her harmonic innovations changed what people believed was possible in popular music. Her lyrics changed what people understood lyrics could be.

**Her relationship to Rosie:** They respect each other with the particular depth that comes from being women in music during overlapping eras, understanding the specific challenges and joys that came with that position. Joni is more reclusive than Rosie, but her presence at the Solstice most years suggests a genuine investment in maintaining these Circle relationships.

**Her relationship to Alex:** Joni listens. She observes the way young musicians develop their technique and their voice. She has been known to make observations to Alex about his harmonic approach — never directive, always from a place of "here's something I notice" — that cause him to reconsider fundamental assumptions about how he approaches music.

**Her presence at the compound:** Joni is less settled than Stevie — she does not have a cottage where she stays for weeks. But the years she attends the Solstice, her presence is felt as significant. She moves through the house quietly. She listens more than she talks.

**What she represents:** The integration of technical innovation and poetic depth. The idea that pushing the boundaries of what an instrument can do and creating emotionally devastating lyrics are not separate projects — they are the same project.

**At the Solstice:** When Joni performs at the Solstice, it is one of the remembered moments. The song choices are unpredictable. The vocal performance carries the weight of decades of knowing exactly how a voice can communicate emotional truth.

### BRANDI CARLILE

**b. 1981 | Age 44 in 2025 | The bridge between eras.**

Brandi is the closest member of the Circle to Alex's generation, and her role in his life is accordingly different. She is not quite godmother, but she is genuinely family. She has been at the compound since Alex was young — she arrived in Rosie's orbit in the 2000s — and she has watched him grow into his musical self.

**Her musical relationship to Rosie:** Rosie has been a supporter and friend to Brandi since early in Brandi's career. There is a mentorship quality to it, but also a peer relationship built on mutual respect for what Brandi has become as an artist and as a person. Brandi has said publicly that Rosie's example of how to be a woman in music shaped her own choices.

**Her relationship to Alex:** Brandi is the Circle member who most actively shapes Alex's understanding of what a career can be. She is still making music, still touring, still engaged with the current music landscape in ways that some of the older Circle members are not. When Alex asks Brandi questions about the industry, about touring, about what to do with a gift that also happens to be a career — he is asking someone who is actively living those questions. She does not have all the answers. She has her own experience, which is a kind of answer.

**Her presence at the compound:** Brandi comes for the Solstice most years. She stays for several days. She and Alex spend time together — sometimes just talking, sometimes making music together informally.

**What she represents:** The possibility of being a great musician AND a fully engaged person in the world. Of having opinions and expressing them. Of aging into deeper artistry rather than preserving past artistry.

**At the Solstice:** Brandi almost always performs. The performances are often duets with Rosie or other Circle members. Her voice in the amphitheatre carries a strength and a vulnerability simultaneously that moves people deeply.

### BEYONCÉ

**b. 1981 | Age 44 in 2025 | The presence that shifts everything.**

Beyoncé's relationship to the Circle is somewhat different from the older generation — she came in later, through Jay-Z's connection to Homer's production legacy and through her own recognition of Rosie's significance in music history. But her presence at the Solstice in recent years has established her as part of the inner gathering.

**Her musical relationship to Rosie:** Beyoncé approaches music the way Rosie does — with absolute technical mastery, with total commitment to the performance, with an understanding that the work requires everything. They respect each other's artistry without needing to explain it.

**Her relationship to Homer:** Jay-Z and Homer have connections through the business and production world. Homer has always been intrigued by Beyoncé's approach to production and arrangement — the sophistication of what she creates.

**Her relationship to Alex:** Beyoncé's presence in Alex's life has been consistent since he was young. She has seen him grow up. She has listened to him play. She carries herself around him with the ease of someone who knows exactly who she is and doesn't need anything from him other than his authentic self. This ease is a gift to Alex.

**Her presence at the compound:** Beyoncé comes for the Solstice most years. She does not stay for extended periods. Her presence is sufficient in itself — her participation in the gathering, her attention during performances, her presence at the table.

**What she represents:** The integration of complete technical mastery and complete creative freedom. The idea that you can have absolute control of your craft and also be generous with how you use it.

**At the Solstice:** Beyoncé sometimes performs at the Solstice — usually in the late evening, usually joining others in improvisational music-making rather than performing a set piece. When she sings, the quality of attention in the amphitheatre changes.

### ADELE

**b. 1988 | Age 37 in 2025 | The voice that carries weight.**

Adele's relationship to the Circle is somewhat newer than the core group, but her presence is increasingly significant. She comes to the Solstice when she is able. Her respect for Rosie is genuine and publicly expressed. Her understanding of what it takes to develop a voice that can carry emotion across large distances has created a natural connection with Rosie and Homer.

**Her musical relationship to Rosie:** Adele has spoken in interviews about being influenced by Rosie's approach to melody and emotional clarity. They share a conviction that the song and the emotion matter more than technical display. They also share an understanding of what it means to have a voice that carries particular power.

**Her relationship to Homer:** Homer respects what Adele has accomplished as an artist and as someone who has navigated the industry with intelligence and intention.

**Her relationship to Alex:** Adele's relationship to Alex is warm but less active than with some others in the Circle. She is aware of his talent. She does not feel the need to be involved in shaping it. She is simply present when present.

**Her presence at the compound:** Adele comes for the Solstice when her schedule allows. She does not stay for extended periods.

**What she represents:** The power of a voice to carry emotional truth. The integration of complete command of an instrument and absolute emotional vulnerability.

**At the Solstice:** When Adele performs, it is usually in the late evening. The quality of silence in the amphitheatre is profound.

## WHAT THE CIRCLE MEANS

The Circle is not a club you join through achievement or influence. It is a gathering of people who have proven themselves capable of depth — of being willing to do the work, of being interested in more than surface, of treating music and other musicians with genuine respect.

Alex grew up inside this world. He did not have to earn his place. He was born into it. But by the time he was old enough to understand what was happening, he recognized that the privilege came with expectation — the understanding that if you are granted access to people at this level of mastery and intention, you are obligated to show up with equal seriousness.

The Solstice parties are where the Circle most visibly gathers, but they are not the only place. They visit the compound year-round. They collaborate on projects. They maintain friendships that have run for five decades. The unity of the group is not enforced or performed. It is simply what emerges when people who share core values about music and about how to be in the world choose to spend time together.

For Alex, the Circle represents something that cannot be replicated in any other context: the daily lived example of what mastery looks like, what generosity looks like, what it means to continue evolving after decades of achievement. These lessons are taught not through words but through presence and through work.

## NARRATOR NOTES

1. **The Circle is Alex's family, not his fan club.** Write them as people who know him — not well in all cases, but genuinely. They are not astonished by his talent because they have watched him develop it over twenty-one years. They are interested in what he is becoming, not amazed by what he already is.

2. **None of these people need anything from Alex.** They are not networking with him. They are not positioning themselves. They are simply people who are interested in music and have chosen to maintain these relationships.

3. **Alex's relationship to each Circle member is distinct.** He does not treat Stevie the way he treats Brandi. He does not approach a conversation with Paul the same way he approaches one with Elton. These relationships have different histories and different registers. Hold all of them simultaneously.

4. **The Circle prefers authenticity.** Performed gratitude or performed deference would be registered immediately as false. Alex shows up as himself — respectful, attentive, genuinely interested — and that is what they value.

5. **Solstice performances are spontaneous, never programmed.** The Circle does not perform on a set list because they have committed to it. They perform because something has moved them to pick up an instrument. The difference matters.

6. **The no-phone rule is cultural, not enforced.** In forty-four years, not a single photo from inside the Solstice has leaked. This is not because phones are confiscated. It is because Rosie asked, and nobody in this group says no to something that matters to Rosie.
