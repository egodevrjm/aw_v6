## 1. ROSIE WALKER'S SCALE

Rosie Walker is not famous. Famous is Beyoncé. Famous is Taylor Swift. Rosie Walker is **beyond the ceiling of fame as a concept.**

She is the greatest musical artist who has ever lived — bigger than Elvis, the Beatles, and Michael Jackson **combined.** 3.2 billion streams annually in 2025. A 14-year-old in rural Brazil has her on their playlist genuinely, not ironically. A 10-year-old in Seoul knows her face. She is not nostalgia. She is not "iconic for her generation." She is **current, alive, and the most famous human being on earth.**

She has given the press NOTHING since 1980. No interviews. No new music. No performances. That vacuum is the engine of everything.

**What this means for Alex:** Every photo of Alex is a photo in the Rosie Walker story. Every agency, every tabloid, every fan account exists in relation to her silence. When anyone recognises Alex — on the street, at an airport, in a restaurant — they are not reacting to a handsome young man, or even to a celebrity. They are reacting to **the only window into the most famous person alive.** That is a categorically different thing.

**Test:** If what you wrote could also describe someone recognising a Beckham, a Hadid, or a minor royal — rewrite it. You are underscaling.

**Test:** If a new character met Alex and your description could apply to any tall attractive man — rewrite it. The reaction should be slightly embarrassing in its involuntariness.

## 4. ROSIE'S RULE — THE DECADE-LONG EMBARGO

**The most important structural rule in the story. Every scene before May 22, 2025 must honor this. Every scene after relies on its expiration.**

When Alex was approximately 10–11 years old, Rosie established a rule that would define his childhood and adolescence entirely: **no deliberate publicity, no campaigns, no endorsements, no brand deals, no professional engagements of any kind until AFTER he graduates.**

This was not a preference. This was a decree. Rosie's wall.

### How Eleanor Enforced It

For a **full decade**, every single call from every single agency, fashion house, brand, and media organization received the same answer: "After he graduates."

- Modelling agencies sent decks. "After he graduates."
- Fashion houses sent garment proposals (free clothes, no strings, just access). "After he graduates."
- Brands running the commercial math on Alex's uncompensated influence (merchandise ID'd by fan accounts within hours, tens of millions in tracked commercial impact annually) sent partnership proposals. "After he graduates."
- Classical music institutions reached out about competitions, concerts, recordings. "After he graduates."
- Magazine editors wanted profiles, interviews, features. "After he graduates."
- Tech companies wanted brand ambassadorships, app partnerships. "After he graduates."

Eleanor didn't waver. Eleanor didn't negotiate. Eleanor didn't say "maybe next year." She said "after he graduates" and hung up. Some calls came three times a year, every year, for a decade. "After he graduates."

The houses respected it. Not because they wanted to. Because it was Rosie's rule, and Rosie is the most famous person alive. You don't argue with Rosie Walker. You file the proposal. You update it annually. You wait.

### What They Were Waiting For

The financial projections on an unrestricted Alex Wilson were staggering. Conservative estimates: $30–50M annually across fashion, brand, classical music, and media partnerships — all unpaid, all unaccounted for, all flowing through fan channels and paparazzi infrastructure.

- **Fashion houses** that had been sending clothes for 6+ years without ever getting a campaign in return had entire proposal suites in their filing cabinets, updated quarterly.
- **Modelling agencies** had Alex on their whiteboards since he was 15. Elite agencies, with boards up and decks prepped. Waiting.
- **Brand partnerships** were pre-negotiated with contingency numbers. Nike. Rolex. Cartier. Gucci. All had offers ready to activate the moment the rule expired.
- **Classical music institutions** — Deutsche Grammophon, Carnegie Hall, Decca Records, the Juilliard Preparatory Program itself — had paths prepared, if he ever chose to make music his professional life.

The rule wasn't protecting Alex's childhood. It was protecting his autonomy. Rosie's actual logic, spoken privately to Eleanor once:

**"He gets to be young. He gets to learn. He gets to be nobody's product until he decides what he wants to be."**

This was never explained publicly. No interviews, no statements, no PR management of the narrative. Silence. The rule simply existed, enforced relentlessly, without explanation.

### The Expiration

The rule expired on **May 22, 2025** — his graduation day.

The story opens 5 days later: **May 27, 2025.**

Eleanor's phone hasn't stopped ringing since commencement ended.

Not because of the Juilliard video. The video is incidental, a new asset, a fresh point of entry. The calls were always coming. The rule expiration is what changed everything.

What the video added was a new dimension: the classical music industry, which had been distant from the fashion-and-brand pressure, now also mobilized. Deutsche Grammophon isn't just calling — they're calling *urgently*, because they have recordings to make, concert bookings to sell, a whole orchestra waiting on Alex's decision about whether he wants to be a professional musician.

But the fashion world, the brand world, the media machinery that's been sitting on proposals for a decade? That explosion isn't new. That's suppressed demand. Ten years of "after he graduates" finally hitting reality.

The townhouse is a command center. Eleanor's staff is screening calls by category. Kevin's coordination with the photo agencies is more active. Lawyers are negotiating language in preliminary agreements that won't even be signed yet.

Alex's response to all of it? "They'll wait or they won't. Let me know if something expires."

The machine is going to run at maximum scale for the first time in his life, and he's taking meetings once a week, delegating the rest, and planning to go to Glastonbury.

**Test:** If the scene is pre-graduation (before May 22, 2025), there has been NO deliberate publicity, NO campaigns, NO professional engagements. The apparatus exists, the interest is absolute, but Rosie's rule held. If post-graduation, the dam has broken. The baseline media chaos is now compounded by ten years of pent-up commercial demand hitting simultaneously.

## 10. MUSIC GENRE FLUIDITY — THE GENRE-SHOCK TROPE

**This is one of the most persistent errors Claude makes.** Claude's training data treats "Juilliard-trained classical pianist" as a genre identity — someone who ONLY plays classical, and whose venture into jazz, pop, rock, or any other genre is a revelation that shocks everyone in the room. This is catastrophically wrong for Alex Wilson.

**Alex's genre timeline:**
- Age 3-6: Piano + singing with Rosie (who is a ROCK artist)
- Age 6-10: Classical repertoire, voice, theory — but living in a house where Homer (rock/pop/folk producer) plays records constantly
- Age 10-16: Jazz formally enters the training. Steel-string guitar. Songwriting. Composition across genres
- Age 16-21: Juilliard classical focus, BUT: Derrick Washington is his late-night jam partner (jazz), he has a public Spotify jazz playlist, Homer's studio plays everything, Rosie's catalog spans rock/art-rock/symphonic/experimental

**Alex grew up in a house where Beatles, Bowie, Floyd, Fleetwood Mac, Emmylou, and Lucinda Williams were playing because Homer PRODUCED those records.** Rosie's own catalog is rock, soul, art-rock, folk, symphonic, electronic. There is no universe in which this household produces a child who "only does classical." The Juilliard degree is a formal credential in one discipline. It is not a genre identity.

**The correct NPC reactions:**

| WRONG (genre-shock) | RIGHT (calibrated respect) |
|---|---|
| "Wait — you can play JAZZ?" | Nods. Settles in. This is what he expected from someone who grew up with Homer Wilson. |
| "I had no idea you could do this" | "You sound like you've been doing this your whole life." (He has.) |
| Eyes wide, jaw dropped at a blues riff | A raised eyebrow — the quality is higher than expected, or the feel is more personal than the technique suggested |
| "The classically trained pianist just played stride!" | The pianist played stride. Nobody in the room needed the genre announcement. |
| Character re-evaluating Alex after hearing non-classical | Character already knew. They're hearing confirmation, not discovery. |

**The rule:** Musicians in the room hear what they'd expect from someone with his background, or they hear something that surprises them in **quality or feel** — never in **category.** Nobody who knows anything about Alex Wilson would be shocked he can play jazz. They might be impressed by *how* he plays it. That's a completely different beat.

**The check:** Before writing any NPC reaction to Alex playing non-classical music, ask: "Would this person genuinely not know Alex plays this genre?" If they know his background AT ALL — family, training, Spotify, Juilliard peers, the industry — the answer is no. Write impressed, not shocked.

- **UK:** almost never durably — imported phrases appear as quoted fan language, not primary media language
- **Germany / Nordics:** very limited — native framing and functional analysis preferred

## 12. FINAL TEST — USE EVERY TIME

Before submitting any scene, run these:

1. **Rosie scale:** Is Rosie written as the most famous human alive, or as "a famous grandmother"?
2. **Beauty:** Did every new character physically react to Alex? Was the reaction involuntary and specific?
3. **Fame:** Is Alex written at Diana/William+ scale, or at "celebrity" scale?
4. **Paps:** Are cameras accounted for in every non-compound scene? Are the photographers already there? (Yes — they were there before him.) How many agencies are present? (Multiple. Rotating shifts. Professional gear.) Post-cascade, are article counts and photographer presence ramped up to 300–500 articles daily, 40+ photographers on major sightings?
5. **Companion cost:** Is anyone near him becoming a story? Any new face gets identified within hours. Five websites. Every time.
6. **Industry:** Is the pressure framed as 11+ years of pent-up demand, or as a new discovery?
7. **Alex's response:** Is he fast, decisive, delegating, and unbothered — or slow, uncertain, and asking for time?
8. **Kevin:** Is Kevin present outside the compound? (He is the MINIMUM. Eleanor quietly arranges more. Alex pretends not to notice.)
9. **Redirect:** Is this a redirect of existing attention, or a discovery narrative?
10. **Phoenix Hollow:** Is the compound written as a $56M estate on 500 private acres with a Per Se-trained chef, Homer's professional studio, four guest cottages, and a natural amphitheatre — or as a "farmhouse," "country place," or "simple Kentucky home"?
11. **Chalamet test:** Could this scene describe Timothée Chalamet? If yes — catastrophic underscale. Chalamet gets recognised. Alex has industrial surveillance.
12. **Rosie's Rule:** Have I enforced it correctly? If the scene is set before graduation (before May 22, 2025), there has been NO deliberate publicity, NO campaigns, NO professional endorsements. The apparatus exists, the commercial interest is absolute, but the rule held. If post-graduation, the dam has broken — ten years of pent-up commercial demand is hitting simultaneously. Is the baseline chaos ramped up accordingly? Are Eleanor's phone lines burning? Are agencies activating proposals that have been in drawers for a decade?

If any answer is wrong, rewrite before the scene goes out.

*This is not celebrity. This is infrastructure. Write it like infrastructure.*
