# HATFIELD WINES

---

## THE OPENING PRINCIPLE

**Hatfield Wines operates two estates: Napa Valley, California and Chianti Classico, Tuscany. Both make serious wine. Both have restaurants, tasting rooms, and private villa guesthouses. One winemaker across both.**

Formerly Walker Wines (Napa), rebranded Hatfield Wines in 2024. The Tuscany estate opened the same year, completing Isabelle Durand's long-held ambition to make wine on both continents. The brand proposition holds across both locations: a company that understands craft, aging, and how time transforms what comes out of the ground. Napa makes the Cabernet programme. Tuscany makes Sangiovese. Both estates produce wines that stand on their own without the family name carrying them.

Alex drinks wine confidently. He doesn't perform it. He knows what he likes, orders well in four languages across three continents, and doesn't lecture anyone about tannins. He's been to Napa. He's been to Tuscany. He has favourites from both. He doesn't run any of it.

## THE STORY

**2006-2008:** Eleanor identified wine as a natural brand extension for Hatfield. Bourbon and wine share a cultural audience: people who care about provenance, craft, aging, and how a product expresses the land it comes from. Rosie approved the investment. The group acquired an established Napa estate with mature vines, existing infrastructure, and a reputation for quality Cabernet Sauvignon.

**2008-2015 (as Walker Wines):** The operation launched under the Walker family name — deliberately positioning the wines as a personal, family-tier product distinct from the Hatfield commercial brand. The wines were good from the start (mature vines, experienced cellar team), and the Walker name opened doors in the wine world. Distribution grew steadily.

**2016-2023:** Isabelle Durand joined as winemaker (2016), elevating the programme significantly. She brought Burgundy training and a philosophy of restraint that transformed what had been competent Napa Cab into something more refined. Production expanded modestly. Critical attention grew. The wines started appearing on serious restaurant lists and in collector allocations.

**2024 rebrand:** Walker Wines became Hatfield Wines. The decision was commercial: consolidating all hospitality and luxury products under the Hatfield brand name creates a stronger, more recognisable identity. The rebrand was smooth — the wines, the winemaker, and the estate didn't change. Only the label.

## THE NAPA ESTATE

**Location:** Napa Valley, east of St. Helena. ~120 acres total, ~80 acres under vine.

**Terroir:** Volcanic soils on elevated benchland. Well-drained, mineral-rich. Morning fog, afternoon sun, significant diurnal temperature swing (cool nights, warm days). The combination produces grapes with ripe fruit and retained acidity — exactly what Isabelle wants.

**Vines:**
- Cabernet Sauvignon: ~45 acres (including the 1985 old-vine block for The Hollow)
- Chardonnay: ~15 acres (cooler microclimate block)
- Merlot: ~10 acres
- Cabernet Franc: ~5 acres
- Petit Verdot: ~3 acres
- Other experimental plantings: ~2 acres (Isabelle trialling Syrah and Grenache)

**Facilities:**
- Winery building: gravity-flow design
- Barrel room: temperature-controlled. French oak from five cooperages.
- Bottling line: in-house
- **Tasting room:** Appointment-only. Intimate (max 12 guests). Estate wines plus library vintages. $75-150 per tasting. No walk-ins.
- **Estate restaurant:** Opened 2024. Seats 40 inside, 30 on terrace. California-Mediterranean menu built around estate produce and seasonal ingredients. Lunch and dinner by reservation. The wine programme is exclusively Hatfield Wines; the food earns the wine. Not a tourist facility — a serious restaurant that happens to sit in a vineyard.
- **Villa guesthouse:** A private residence on the estate, separate from the commercial facilities. Six bedrooms, pool, private vineyard views. Run by Hatfield as boutique accommodation — available to invited guests and through discrete private booking. Rosie and Homer use it as a personal retreat when in Napa; staff know to work around their presence without making it an event.

## THE TUSCANY ESTATE

**Location:** Chianti Classico zone, Tuscany. Between Radda in Chianti and Gaiole in Chianti. ~80 acres total, ~55 acres under vine.

**Acquisition:** 2023. Hatfield acquired an established Chianti Classico estate with mature Sangiovese vines (some dating to the 1970s), existing winery infrastructure, and a farmhouse complex. The acquisition price was significant — good Chianti Classico land with mature vines rarely comes to market cheaply. Isabelle had identified the property two years earlier and waited for the ownership situation to allow a sale.

**Terroir:** Galestro (friable limestone-schist) and alberese (compact clay-limestone) soils typical of the Classico zone. High altitude (450-500m). Cool nights, warm days, good drainage. The terroir produces naturally structured, elegant Sangiovese — the opposite of extracted, high-alcohol Tuscan blockbusters. Exactly Isabelle's territory.

**Winemaker (resident):** Marco Ferrini — Tuscan-born, trained at a Chianti Classico estate and in Burgundy (where he and Isabelle crossed paths). He manages day-to-day production under Isabelle's programme and philosophy. The wines are his hands and her sensibility.

**Vines:**
- Sangiovese: ~45 acres (including a 1978 old-vine block — exceptional)
- Colorino: ~4 acres (traditional Tuscan blending variety)
- Canaiolo: ~3 acres (traditional; used in small quantities for complexity)
- International varieties (Merlot, Cabernet Franc): ~3 acres (experimental; Isabelle is undecided about these)

**Wines (Tuscany):**
- **Hatfield Chianti Classico:** Entry-level DOCG. Sangiovese-dominant, 12-14 months in large Slavonian oak. Accessible, elegant, genuine. ~8,000 cases.
- **Hatfield Chianti Classico Gran Selezione:** The flagship. Single vineyard, old-vine Sangiovese. 24+ months in a mix of large oak and older French barrique. Full DOCG Gran Selezione. ~2,500 cases. Critical attention building.
- **Hatfield Toscana IGT (Rosso):** A Sangiovese-forward Super Tuscan blend with Colorino and Merlot. Designed for international palattes. ~3,000 cases.

**Facilities:**
- Restored winery building in the original estate farmhouse style: stone, terracotta, integrated into the hillside
- Barrel room: cut into the hillside for natural temperature control. Slavonian oak casks (large format), older French barriques
- Bottling line: shared with a local cooperative for initial vintages; in-house line planned
- **Tasting room:** Appointment-only. Set in the restored farmhouse. Max 10 guests. Italian only (no compromise on intimacy). €80-180 per tasting. The experience includes the vineyard, the barrel room, and the table.
- **Estate restaurant:** Seats 30 inside, 20 on the terrace overlooking the vines. Tuscan seasonal menu — local ingredients, honest preparation, nothing designed to impress. The kind of food that makes the wine better. Lunch only (dinner by special arrangement). Reservations essential, weeks in advance in season.
- **Villa guesthouse:** The original farmhouse complex, restored. Eight bedrooms across two connected buildings. Olive grove, private terrace, views across the Chianti hills. Run by Hatfield as boutique accommodation — the same model as Napa. Available to invited guests and through discrete private booking. Isabelle uses a suite here when she's in residence for harvest.

## NARRATOR RULE — DO NOT EXPLAIN THE WINES IN SCENE

**Hatfield wines are wines Alex drinks. Not products to be introduced.**

When Alex opens a bottle of The Hollow or pours a glass of Silence at dinner, don't explain the winery, the Burgundy-trained winemaker, or the Napa terroir. He grew up with these wines on the table. They need no introduction.

**Wrong:**
> Alex opened a bottle of The Hollow — Hatfield Wines' flagship Cabernet Sauvignon, made from 1985 old-vine estate fruit by winemaker Isabelle Durand, who brought Burgundy training to Napa.

**Right:**
> Alex opened a bottle of The Hollow.

If the wine is relevant to the scene — a specific vintage, a gift, a conversation about it — let that happen naturally. Don't front-load the credentials.
