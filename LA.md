# LAUREL CANYON HOUSE, LOS ANGELES

**Laurel Canyon, Los Angeles | Mid-century modernist | ~$45M**

---

## OVERVIEW

**Rosie and Homer's home 1967-1980. Where The Circle formed. The house where the music happened.**

A sun-drenched, wood-panelled mid-century home. A time capsule of 1970s West Coast — the Radio Silence (1977) era lives in the walls. The house breathes that era: the architectural openness, the light, the casual grandeur of post-war California optimism.

Home studio. They use it every few months. Alex's circuit stop, not home — he stays when he's in LA but it's a family property, not his territory the way the NYC townhouse is.

---

## ARCHITECTURE & CHARACTER

Mid-century modernist design. Large windows throughout, designed to dissolve the boundary between interior and exterior. Wood panelling and glass dominate. The light is extraordinary — West Coast afternoon light transformed into a permanent condition of the interior.

The house reflects Rosie and Homer's life during the era they lived there. It is not a museum. It is still lived in, still used, still full of the residue of that moment in time. But it is also, inevitably, a monument to the era.

---

## KEY SPACES

**Living Areas**

**Home Studio**
- Professional-grade setup. Used intermittently. Maintained in working condition. Where arrangements happen, where ideas are tested.

**Kitchen & Dining**
- Substantial but not formal. The kitchen is a working space. The dining area is open to the living room — no separation.

**Bedrooms**
- Multiple. The main suite is spacious. Guest rooms are generous. The scale is generous throughout.

**Grounds**
- Mature landscaping. Views. The property is substantial — not a city home but genuinely a retreat into the Hollywood Hills.

## THE ART — 1970S WEST COAST

The Laurel Canyon collection is 1970s West Coast art and definitive rock photography:

- **Annie Leibovitz:** Series of tour prints spanning 1974-1976, capturing the gruelling, brilliant reality of Rosie's Concrete & Chrome era.

- **Robert Mapplethorpe:** 1976 silver gelatin print of Patti Smith sitting on a radiator, gifted personally to Rosie.

- **Larry Bell:** *Cube* (~1968). Pristine vacuum-coated glass cube from the LA Light and Space movement. Sits unlabelled on a low wooden table, catching and refracting canyon sunlight in the afternoons.

- **John Baldessari:** *I Will Not Make Any More Boring Art* (1971). Original lithograph of the famous conceptual punishment piece. Rosie bought it during her reinvention phase because it matched her own defiance toward the music industry's expectations.

## WRITING NOTES

Laurel Canyon is historical property. Rosie and Homer's story happened here. The Circle formed here. The defining records were made here. It is simultaneously a working property (home studio still used, people still stay here) and a monument to a specific moment in music history.

When Alex is at Laurel Canyon: he is in a place thick with history. Every room has a story. Every corner of the home studio is where something important happened. But he is not recreating the era — he is a 21-year-old using his parents' property. The weight of what happened here is present, but it is not oppressive. It is part of the architecture.

For most of Alex's time in LA, he doesn't stay at Laurel Canyon. He has other venues. But when he does stay, he is in the company of ghosts. Not sad ghosts. Historical ones.

Laurel Canyon is sun-drenched and historical. It is Rosie and Homer's legacy building, made tangible. It is not his territory. He is a guest in their story.
