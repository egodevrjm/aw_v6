# MERCER STREET TOWNHOUSE, NYC

**West 70s, Upper West Side | 6-story brownstone + basement | ~14,000 sqft | 8 bedrooms | ~$22M current valuation**

---

## OVERVIEW

**Five blocks from Lincoln Center.** AW Estates (subsidiary of AW Holdings LLC) asset. Of all the properties, this is most purely Alex's — the only one he has built from scratch. Alex owns this property through his personal entity, AW Estates.

Rosie bought the townhouse in the late 1960s during her early NYC career — pre-Homer, pre-London, pre-everything. She was in her early twenties, just breaking through. The UWS was her first serious property purchase. Then life moved: London (Homer, Abbey Road), Laurel Canyon (the 70s), Kentucky (retirement, 1980). The NYC house sat there for decades. Walker Holdings maintained it. It got used for the odd week — Grammys, business, Met Gala trips. Nobody ever really lived in it. No personal touches accumulated because nobody was there long enough to care.

When Juilliard started at 18, the townhouse transferred into AW Holdings and Alex commissioned a **full architectural refit** — gut renovation of the interiors while preserving the brownstone bones. New kitchen, new bathrooms, basement conversion (gym, pool, steam room), climate-controlled dressing room, soundproofing throughout (Homer's one contribution — he insisted, even though he'd never use it). The renovation took the best part of a year, with Alex living through parts of it during freshman year. Cost: ~$3-4M from the fund.

By now, this house has had three years of Alex living in it and shaping it. Every room reflects a decision he made between 18 and 21. It is not inherited atmosphere — it is built atmosphere. The contrast with Kensington (where Alex exists within Homer's space) is total.

---

## STAFF

A **private company** handles cleaning, maintenance, and cooking for Alex when he's in residence. Not live-in staff — they come and go. The house is kept ready at all times (Walker Holdings / AW Holdings contracts), but when Alex is away for months it's essentially empty except for periodic maintenance visits. This is different from the compound (Maria, Dale Shackleford, Loretta — full-time, family) and Kensington (Mrs Gable — a fixture). NYC is professional, discreet, impersonal. Alex doesn't know their surnames and they don't expect him to.

**Kevin Marsh** — Close Protection Officer — maintains a separate entrance and security station in the basement when Alex is in residence (bed, bathroom, monitors). Not luxurious, not cramped. Functional.

## PARLOR LEVEL (FIRST FLOOR)

## ART — MAIN SPACES

The inherited Walker Holdings art (Rosie's 1960s-80s acquisitions) anchors the lower floors — staggering pieces from the downtown NYC scene and American minimalism that came with the property. Alex's personal collection (see ALEX_COLLECTION.md §II) fills the upper floors and the spaces between. The combined effect is an extraordinary private gallery that someone happens to live in.

### Parlor Level

- **Donald Judd:** *Untitled* (1968). The "stack." Dominates the salon. See above.
- **Jean-Michel Basquiat:** *Untitled* canvas, 1983. Parlor level, facing the Steinway.
- **Richard Prince:** *Untitled (Joke)* painting, late 1980s. Entryway — dry, cynical humour. The first thing you see walking in.

### Second Floor (Library / Study)

- **Cindy Sherman:** *Untitled Film Still*. Above the reading chair.
- **Keith Haring:** Original chalk drawing, bought directly from the artist's studio. Library wall.

### Alex's Personal Collection (Placed Throughout)

- **Yoshitomo Nara** — *Sleepless Night (Sitting)*. Hallway, third floor.
- **Christopher Wool** — untitled word painting. Library, second floor.
- **Mark Bradford** — mixed media. Parlor level, opposite the Judd.
- **Agnes Martin** — screenprint. Alex's bedroom, fourth floor. Opposite the bed — the first thing he sees in the morning.
- **Joan Mitchell** — lithograph. Second floor sitting room.
- **Raymond Pettibon** — ink and watercolour. Fifth floor landing.
- **Brice Marden** — etching. Library, second floor.
- **Elizabeth Peyton** — small portrait. Alex's bedroom, fourth floor.
- **KAWS** — figure and screenprint. Sixth floor den.

### Alex's Suite (Fourth Floor)

- **Alexander McQueen Sketch (1999):** Directly above the bed. Visceral charcoal and watercolour for the *No. 13* runway. Alex bought it himself at auction — not inherited. Bridges his appreciation for meticulous construction and dark romance.
- **Cosima's "Sound & Bone" Sculpture:** Massive mixed-media piece created specifically for him by Cosima. Large-scale cyanotype layered with shattered reclaimed piano wire and resin — a visual representation of violent, beautiful sound. Leans casually against the wall near the music room door. Matches the stark energy of the Juilliard years.
- **Chopin Autograph Manuscript:** Tiny museum-framed scrap of parchment — three measures of a Nocturne in Chopin's own hand. Rosie gave it the day his Juilliard acceptance arrived. Quietly on his desk.

## WRITING NOTES

This is Alex's property in the fullest sense. Every aesthetic choice, every piece of art, every room's function — built between 18 and 21 by a young man who knew exactly what he wanted.

When writing scenes at Mercer Street: Alex navigates this space with full ownership. He knows where things are. He chooses which floors to use. He decides who comes here and who doesn't. This is the one property where he is unambiguously in control.
