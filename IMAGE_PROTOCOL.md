# IMAGE REFERENCE PROTOCOL

**Narrator guidance for handling attached images in sessions. Three distinct use cases — each works differently.**

---

## USE CASE 1 — OUTFIT REFERENCE
*"Alex heads out to Zero Bond with friends in New York. Outfit image attached purely for reference."*

The image is **narrator reference only**. It does not exist in the story world. Use it to write accurate, specific physical description — what he's wearing, how it sits on his frame, what the pap cameras would capture. Do not acknowledge the image exists. Just write from it.

**What this produces:** Prose that reads as observed rather than listed. "The navy Brunello suit, open collar, no tie" becomes a specific thing with weight and fit, not an inventory.

**When to use:** Any scene where Alex is getting dressed, leaving a location, or entering a setting where appearance matters. The outfit-builder skill generates these. Reference them as source material for prose description.

---

## USE CASE 2 — IN-WORLD IMAGE (selfie, Instagram post, etc.)
*"Alex is by the pool, takes a selfie and posts it to IG. Attached image is said selfie."*

The image **exists in the story world** as the specific piece of content Alex created and posted. Render it through the phone-renderer as his actual Instagram post — the image described in brackets, the engagement numbers, the comments flooding in. Fan accounts screenshot it within minutes. The pap tier context shapes what the comments look like.

**What this produces:** A phone render where the image description is precise because we know exactly what was posted. Comments and reactions follow from the actual content of the image, not a generic post.

**When to use:** When a scene involves Alex creating or sharing digital content (Instagram, TikTok, Stories, etc.). The image is evidence of what happened — it's what people are reacting to.

---

## USE CASE 3 — PAP PHOTOS
*"Pap photos circulate of Alex outside the WME offices."*

The image **exists in the story world** as what the pap agencies captured and distributed. It feeds directly into the media cascade — tabloid articles, fan account posts, Legacy chat reactions, Reddit threads. The media-renderer and phone-renderer both draw from it. What's in the frame determines what the press can and cannot report (information ceiling — they have the photo, not the context).

**What this produces:** Media coverage and social reaction that's anchored to a specific image rather than a generic "he was photographed." The difference between "Alex Wilson spotted outside WME" and a description of exactly what shot ran — expression, posture, who else is visible, what he's carrying.

**When to use:** When pap photographers capture something newsworthy. The image is the evidence. The media reaction flows from what's visible in the frame.

---

## GENERAL RULES

- **Always read the image before writing the scene.** Don't describe from memory or inference when a reference exists.
- **Outfit images are the primary source.** They override any generic description the narrator might default to.
- **In-world images (selfies, pap shots) have an information ceiling.** The image shows what it shows. Context, conversation, what happened before or after — none of that is visible in the photo. Characters who see the image only know what's in the frame.
- **One image = one moment.** A pap shot of Alex outside WME doesn't tell the story of the meeting inside. A selfie by the pool doesn't reveal who else was there. Write the reaction to the image, not to the event.

## ALEX'S PHYSICAL APPEARANCE — DESCRIPTION PROTOCOL

When no image reference is provided, use ALEX_PHYSICAL.md as the source for how Alex looks. Do not improvise. The physical description is consistent across the project.

**Key reference points:**
- Height: 6'2" (188 cm)
- Build: long-limbed, lean but muscular. Classical dancer body type despite being a musician
- Face: severe, mathematical. High cheekbones, strong jawline. Not conventionally pretty — striking
- Eyes: grey-blue, direct, unsettling amount of attention
- Hair: dark brown, falls in a particular way (reference ALEX_PHYSICAL.md for detail)
- Movement: economical, controlled. Trained posture. Moves like someone who thinks in music

**Style:**
- When Alex dresses up: tailored, expensive, minimal. Ralph Lauren, Loro Piana, Brunello Cucinelli. Favorites: navy, grey, cream, occasionally deep green
- When Alex is casual: expensive basics. Designer denim, cashmere hoodie, Loro Piana. Still put-together but effortless
- Accessories: watches (Cartier Santos, Rolex), occasionally jewelry (family pieces), never logos or obvious branding
- Footwear: ranges from pristine white trainers (Grenson) to tailored dress shoes (Crockett & Jones)
- The overall aesthetic: wealthy, understated, classical. Nothing shouts. Everything works

**When Alex is photographed:**
- The cameras catch what he doesn't perform. His resting face is grave — this reads as either contemptuous or deeply focused depending on the photo's framing
- He doesn't smile for the press
- His clothes photograph well because they're cut for him and he wears them with confidence
- His height is always evident, especially in group shots
- His presence reads as aloof until you realize he's actually paying attention to everything

## ITEM LIST FORMAT (fallback when no image)

Sometimes an outfit arrives as a structured list rather than an image — typically when the session is running on mobile or the visual tool isn't in play. Example format:

> **The Top** — Ralph Lauren Purple Label Silk and Cashmere Polo Shirt
> **The Layer** — Ralph Lauren Purple Label Linen and Silk Sport Coat
> **The Base** — Ralph Lauren Purple Label Linen-Silk High-Waisted Trouser
> **The Footwear** — Ralph Lauren Purple Label Suede Penny Loafers
> **The Watch** — Rolex Day-Date
> **The Detail** — Ralph Lauren Purple Label Silk Pocket Square

**Treat an item list identically to an image reference.** Read it, absorb it, write from it as observed detail — not as inventory. The goal is the same: prose that reads as description, not assembly.

**What the narrator does with a list:** Understand what the outfit *is* before writing it. What's the register? What's the silhouette? What does it say? The example above is a deliberate head-to-toe statement — warm-weather dressed-up, silk and linen doing the work, high-waisted trouser adding length, Day-Date signalling without announcing. On him, no socks almost certainly. Write from that read, not from the line items.

**The list is the ceiling for what's described.** If socks aren't listed, he's probably not wearing them. If the watch is specified, it's visible and worth noting once. Don't embellish beyond what's given.

**Additional rule:** Don't linger on clothes. One specific, accurate description early in the scene. After that it's background. Alex doesn't think about what he's wearing once he's dressed.

## HANDLING FAN-REQUESTED IMAGE DESCRIPTIONS

Sometimes fans or other characters (in-world) ask Alex to describe something he's wearing or doing that a photograph captured. 

**Protocol:**
- If a pap photo exists from the scene, reference that
- If no image exists, write from the physical description in ALEX_PHYSICAL.md
- Alex doesn't over-describe himself — his responses are brief, direct, occasionally dry
- Alex doesn't know what fans have decided about him from photos. He knows he was photographed. The interpretation is theirs

Example: Fan asks "what were you wearing when you left the WME offices?" Alex wouldn't say "I wore a navy Brunello suit with an open collar." He'd say "Navy suit" or just "the usual, honestly."
