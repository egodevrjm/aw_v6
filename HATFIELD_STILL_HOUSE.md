# STILL HOUSE RESTAURANT

---

## THE OPENING PRINCIPLE

**Still House is a James Beard Award-winning restaurant adjacent to a bourbon distillery in Bardstown, Kentucky — and it has no business being this good in a town of 14,000 people.**

That's the point. Still House exists because Hatfield Group had the capital, the taste, and the patience to build a destination restaurant in bourbon country rather than a distillery cafeteria. It draws diners from Louisville (45 minutes), Lexington (50 minutes), and increasingly from much further. The James Beard recognition is not honorary — the kitchen is doing serious, original work with Kentucky ingredients and bourbon influence.

**Still House is now two locations.** Bardstown, Kentucky (this file) and Perthshire, Scotland (at the Hatfield Scotland distillery — see HATFIELD_SCOTLAND.md). Same brand philosophy — a serious restaurant on distillery grounds — but the Scotland location has its own chef (Catriona Reid), its own menu (the Scottish larder, French technique), and its own identity. Marcus Boyle recommended Catriona personally. They are not the same restaurant. They share a name, a standard, and a commitment to not being a tourist facility.

Alex has eaten at Bardstown since childhood. It's where the family goes for special dinners when they're at the compound. Homer's 80th birthday dinner was here. It's not Alex's regular haunt (that's Hatfield Bar & Grill Lexington) — it's the place for occasions.

## THE MENU

Still House runs a seasonal menu that changes substantially every 6-8 weeks, with individual dishes rotating more frequently based on what's available from farms and foragers. The core identity doesn't change: Kentucky ingredients, modern technique, bourbon where it earns its place.

### Signature Dishes (recurring across seasons)

**Bourbon Barrel-Aged Beef** — Dry-aged ribeye or strip finished in a bourbon barrel aging environment (not soaked in bourbon — exposed to the atmosphere inside a used Hatfield barrel). Subtle sweetness, caramelised exterior, served with seasonal accompaniment. The dish that most food writers mention. $68.

**Smoked Lamb Shoulder** — Kentucky lamb, smoked over bourbon barrel staves. 12-hour preparation. Pulled at table. Served with pickled vegetables and corn bread that's nothing like the corn bread you're expecting. $52.

**Country Ham & Fig** — Kentucky country ham (cured 18+ months), grilled Mission figs, aged goat cheese from a local dairy, benne seed crackers. Appetiser. $24. Deceptively simple. Execution is everything.

**The Bourbon Flight Course** — On the tasting menu only. A single dish designed around a specific Hatfield expression. Changes with the tasting menu rotation. Recent example: Cave Select-infused consommé with smoked quail and black truffle. The bourbon is the ingredient, not the pairing.

**Pawpaw Dessert** — Seasonal (September-October only). Kentucky pawpaw fruit — native, obscure, custardy. Marcus turns it into a tart, a mousse, a sauce depending on the year. Food media goes wild every autumn. Locals just eat it.

### The Bourbon Pairing

The tasting menu offers an optional bourbon pairing ($85) curated in collaboration with Carolyn Hatfield-Moore. Not wine pairing — bourbon pairing. Seven courses, each matched with a specific Hatfield expression or preparation (neat, rocks, in a cocktail element, as an ingredient). The pairing is educational without being lecturing — the server explains the match, you taste, you understand. This programme alone generates significant press attention.

### Wine Programme

Still House has a serious wine list despite the bourbon focus. ~250 selections, strong on American wines (Napa, Oregon, Finger Lakes) and European classics. The sommelier works with the kitchen to offer both wine and bourbon pairings. No one is forced into bourbon. But most people try it.

## THE PHYSICAL SPACE

**Building:** Purpose-built (2016) adjacent to the distillery, designed to complement the existing architecture. Stone foundation, timber frame, large windows overlooking distillery grounds and Kentucky farmland. Single storey with high ceilings. Not a barn conversion — a modern building with traditional materials.

**Main dining room (45 seats):** Open, light-filled during sunset service, intimate as darkness falls. Tables spaced generously — no elbow-to-elbow dining. White tablecloths, quality glassware, understated. The view does the work.

**Private dining room (12 seats):** Separated from the main room by a heavy sliding door. Used for family dinners, bourbon industry events, special occasions. The Wilson family uses this room. Reservable by anyone.

**Chef's counter (8 seats):** Bar seating facing the open kitchen. The premium experience — watch Marcus and his team work. Tasting menu only at the counter. Books out weeks ahead.

**Bar/lounge (adjacent):** Small bar area for pre-dinner drinks and guests waiting for tables. Bourbon-forward. Not a full bar programme — Still House is a restaurant, not a bar. Guests drink before dinner, with dinner, and leave. The bar isn't the destination.

**The porch:** Covered outdoor space overlooking the distillery grounds. Pre-dinner bourbon and appetisers in warm weather. The view across bourbon country — rolling farmland, rickhouse silhouettes, Kentucky sunset — is one of the best dining settings in the state.

## THE GUEST EXPERIENCE

**Who eats here:**
- Bourbon tourists who've discovered it (Still House is increasingly the reason people come to Bardstown, not just a bonus after the distillery tour)
- Louisville and Lexington food people on a destination dinner drive
- Food writers and critics on the bourbon trail or covering Kentucky food
- Bourbon industry people (distillers, brand directors, distributors) hosting clients or celebrating
- Hatfield Distillery Inn guests (easy walk — the restaurant is adjacent)
- Occasional national food pilgrims who saw the James Beard win or the Bon Appétit piece
- Wilson family for private occasions

**What the experience feels like:**
- Arrival: farmland, distillery grounds visible, rickhouse silhouettes against the sky. You're in bourbon country and the restaurant doesn't pretend otherwise.
- Entry: warm welcome, professional. Not formal in a stiff way — professional in a Kentucky way. The hostess probably grew up within 30 miles of here.
- Dinner: paced well (90-120 minutes for à la carte, 2-2.5 hours for tasting menu). Each course arrives with enough explanation to be informative without being performative. The food is the thing.
- Bourbon pairing: if you're doing it, this is where the meal becomes something you'll talk about for years. The Cave Select course — whatever Marcus has built around it this season — is usually the moment.
- Departure: you're on a country road in the dark, driving back to the Hatfield Inn or to Louisville, and you're thinking about what just happened.

## NARRATOR RULE — DO NOT EXPLAIN STILL HOUSE IN SCENE

**Still House is a restaurant. Reference it like any other restaurant.**

When a scene is set at Still House or someone mentions it, don't flag the James Beard Award, the Bardstown location, or Marcus Boyle's credentials. People eating there know where they are. Let the food and the room do the work.

**Wrong:**
> They had dinner at Still House — the James Beard Award-winning restaurant on the Hatfield distillery grounds, run by chef Marcus Boyle, the only fine dining destination in Bardstown.

**Right:**
> They had dinner at Still House. Marcus had put together something with the spring lamb.

The credentials belong in this file. The scene earns them through specificity, not explanation.
