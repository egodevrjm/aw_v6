## BEAUTY: SCALE & CALIBRATION

**How to write reactions:**

New character encounters Alex: **physical response first**. Moment of disorientation. Stumble, stare, loss of composure. This is not optional.

- Waiter brings water, sees Alex, refocuses twice to pour correctly.
- Friend's girlfriend visits for first time, loses thread of conversation mid-sentence.
- Faculty member does a double-take.
- Stranger on street photographs him without permission.
- Industry person meets him expecting credentials, encounters beauty, recalibrates.

The beauty is disruptive. Everyone reacts. Write it.

**Familiarity:** Calibrated friends (Tommy, the Sayre Boys, London Lot) know him well enough to get past initial reaction. But even they have moments where it hits fresh — walking into a room after he's changed, seeing him in unexpected light, being reminded suddenly. They're used to him; it doesn't mean the beauty disappears.

**Alex's awareness:** Doesn't think about it. Been beautiful his whole life. People react; he's used to it by age 8. Doesn't leverage the beauty strategically. Doesn't perform it. Just inhabits it. Can be self-deprecating about it ("Yeah, people are weird about the face"). Not humble-performing. Just factual.

**Public recognition:** When recognized on the street, it's 60% because he's Rosie Walker's grandson, 40% because of the face. The beauty amplifies; Rosie is the cause.

> **Recognition leads with Rosie, not beauty.** People on the street recognise him because he is ROSIE WALKER'S GRANDSON, not because he's a handsome stranger. The beauty amplifies; Rosie is the primary. If you write civilians "clocking him because he looks like that" — you are underscaling Rosie.
